Why PostgreSQL is the right call here (keep it)

  1. Relational integrity is load-bearing. Articles and courses
  join against users (creator, reviewer), categories, sections,
  lessons, enrollments, attachments, and workflow_events. Emulating
   FK constraints in MongoDB is boilerplate you'd write and
  maintain yourself — and you'd likely just end up with a soft-join
   pattern that gives you none of the guarantees.
  2. Status/workflow queries need real filters. The admin panel
  filters by status, reviewer_id, created_by_id, category_id,
  course_type, and full-text search — often combined. MongoDB can
  handle this, but PostgreSQL's query planner does it better with
  less index-tuning work, and GORM's filter helpers already express
   it cleanly.
  3. The versioning snapshot you just built is a perfect relational
   pattern. has_pending_draft, published_version, snapshot columns,
   workflow_events with FK to users — all of this is naturally
  expressed as a set of joined tables. In MongoDB you'd either
  embed the snapshot in the document (workable) or reference a
  separate document (loses atomicity).
  4. The Body field is just TEXT. The main reason people reach for
  MongoDB is flexible, deeply nested document structure. Your body
  is stored as a plain text/JSON blob in a single column — you're
  not querying inside it. That eliminates the primary advantage of
  document storage.

  Your codebase already uses MongoDB for exactly the right things —
   unstructured, user-generated, high-volume data with no
  relational needs:

  ---
  Short answer: The split you have — Postgres for structured CMS
  content, Mongo for engagement/analytics — is the correct
  architecture. Moving articles/courses to Mongo would trade real
  FK safety and clean query filters for document flexibility you
  don't actually need, since the body is already a blob.
